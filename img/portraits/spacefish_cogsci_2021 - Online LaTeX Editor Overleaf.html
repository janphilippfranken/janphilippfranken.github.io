\subsubsection{Directed (social) Edges}
There exist several approaches for modeling how people
might signal their beliefs in social networks.
Examples include binary beliefs based on deterministic
decision thresholds \cite{pilditch2017opinion},
continuous beliefs \cite{lorenz2007continuous},
probability matching \cite{whalen2018sensitivity}, or
the communication of full probability densities
\cite{frankenbelief}. Here, we modeled belief signals
as a combination of a a direction (planet is
predominantly \{red: 1--3, blue 4--6/}) and confidence
in this assessment. We assume these beliefs and
confidences are a function of an agent's $\alpha$ and
$\beta$ parameters. The belief is given by the mean of
the beta distribution:
\begin{equation}
Belief = \begin{cases}
red \text{ if } \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} >
    0.5 \\
blue \text{ if }\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} <
    0.5
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
and confidence based on the cumulative density
function of the beta distribution $F(x; \alpha,\beta)$
(i.e.,$F(0.5; \alpha,\beta) -.5$ for the strength of a
blue belief).
For a concrete example, imagine that $B$ observes a
red fish at $t = 2$. Starting with an initially
uniform belief and incorporating one red pseudo count,
their new belief is $Beta(2,1)$. Based on the above
mechanism, they will thus signal a $red$ belief with a
confidence (strength) of $0.5-F(0.5;2,1)$ = 0.25 to
$C$ at $t = 3$. Assuming that $C$ had a uniform belief
of $Beta(1,1)$, their new belief after incorporating
$B$'s signal (and without any other evidence) will be
$Beta(1.25,1)$ by the end of $t = 3$. For a ``neutral
belief'' (i.e., $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} =
0.5$), the cumulative density of the beta distribution
on either side of the mean is 0.5, meaning that a
belief signal had a confidence of 0 and thus no
influence on another agent's belief.
